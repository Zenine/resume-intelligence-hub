## Example 1: Leading the team through a major crisis (format reference, anonymized)

**Answers questions like**:
- Tell me about the biggest challenge you handled
- How do you make decisions under uncertainty
- Give me an example of your leadership

**S (Situation)** — Q3 2022, a sudden regulatory change meant 60% of my product's planned features had to be rewritten in 3 weeks. My team of 12 was already burnt out from two months of overtime.

**T (Task)** — Ship on time, protect the team, keep the customer's confidence. The deadline was hard; the customer was a major paying account that couldn't take a delay; the team couldn't take another grind.

**A (Action)** — 1) Negotiated with my boss to push non-committed features to next quarter, freeing 30% of bandwidth; 2) redistributed work based on individual state and forced the two most-exhausted teammates to rotate out; 3) switched from daily 15-min standups to two sync points per week to protect focus time; 4) proactively briefed the customer on progress and risk, earning a two-week buffer.

**R (Result)** — Shipped the core compliant version in 3 weeks; the customer renewed; no one on the team quit; the next quarter's performance rating was higher than the previous one. I learned that "fight the fire first, retrospect after" sustains the team better than "blame yourself and grind harder."

**Backup material**:
- Data points: projected delay cost ~$120K, actual loss $0, team NPS recovered from -20 to +15
- Reflection: I should have gone to my boss for a buffer earlier — not one week before the deadline

## Example 2: Turning around a rejected proposal (format reference, anonymized)

**Answers questions like**:
- Who's the toughest stakeholder you've had to persuade
- Tell me about a failure
- How do you handle disagreement

**S (Situation)** — Pitched a new business direction to the exec team; rejected unanimously by 3 VPs on the grounds of "market isn't big enough."

**T (Task)** — Either drop it or find evidence that would change their view. I picked the second.

**A (Action)** — 1) Got coffee 1:1 with each rejector to surface their real concerns (turned out one VP was worried about resource contention, not market size); 2) re-framed the pitch around three customer interviews, changing "market size" to "run a 3-month pilot with the existing team, no new resources"; 3) re-pitched in a smaller meeting with only the decision-maker, cut the deck to 5 slides.

**R (Result)** — Got 3-month pilot approval. The pilot landed its first internal seed customer in Q2, became a standalone business line in Q3, and passed $1M ARR in year one.

**Backup material**:
- Data points: pilot budget $40K, year-1 revenue $1.5M, team grew from 3 to 14 in year 2
- Reflection: I first read "rejected" as "failed". It wasn't — it was "information incomplete." The real failure would have been giving up
